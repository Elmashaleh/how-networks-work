01
The basic question

What has to be true for a message to survive the trip?

A network is just a group of devices that can exchange data. Two laptops on a cable — a network. Your phone and a billion other devices on the internet — also a network, just a much bigger one. What should sound impossible is that it works perfectly, in milliseconds, between machines that have never met.

Computers don't really "talk." They follow a script so rigid that two devices built decades apart, by companies that never spoke to each other, still understand one another. Strip away the jargon, and any conversation — human or machine — needs exactly four things:

  • 01 Someone to send the message
  • 02 Someone to receive it
  • 03 An address that says exactly where it's going
  • 04 A way to know it actually arrived
Everything else in networking — IP, MAC, routers, TCP — is just a more disciplined version of something you already do every time you mail a letter.
02
The mail analogy

You already know this system. You've used it your whole life.

To send a friend a letter you need an envelope with something written inside, your friend's address in a format the postal system understands, a mailbox to drop it in, and — if it matters — a tracking number so you know it arrived instead of vanishing.

That's four things again, in disguise. Computers do exactly this, billions of times a second — they just call the pieces different names.

Sending a letter

  • Envelope with a message inside
  • A clear street address
  • Trucks & sorting centers you never see
  • A tracking number, if it matters

Sending data

  • A packet with your data inside
  • An IP address
  • Routers you never see
  • A delivery receipt, always
03
The address problem

Every device carries two different answers at once.

The IP address is where you live right now — assigned by whichever network you're on, and it changes the moment you switch networks. The MAC address is who you are, stamped into the hardware at the factory, like a passport photo. It never changes, no matter which Wi-Fi you join.

Try it on the right: move to a new address and watch which one actually changes.

The sticky-note address label gets swapped every time you move. The passport photo inside never does — it was never the postal system's to change in the first place.

04
The path nobody plans

No single office knows the whole journey.

Every hop just asks one question: "Based on the address on this packet, where's the next best stop to get it closer to home?" — hands it off, and lets the next stop decide. There is no master map of the internet.

05
Why your message gets torn apart

A giant, unbroken message is fragile. A thousand small ones aren't.

Send a photo, and before it leaves your device it's torn into packets — each one labeled with which message it belongs to and where it falls in the sequence. They don't even have to take the same road, and they don't even have to arrive in order.

It's less like mailing one heavy envelope, and more like tearing a letter into numbered strips and trusting the system to deliver every one — so you can tape it back together exactly as it was written.

06
The receipt nobody asked for

Nothing is ever assumed delivered until someone says so.

Every packet gets a receipt. If a confirmation doesn't arrive in a fraction of a second, the sender doesn't shrug — it resends the exact same packet, because a missing receipt could mean a missing piece. This relentless bookkeeping is, in essence, what TCP does.
